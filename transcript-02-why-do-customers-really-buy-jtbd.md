## Core Thesis of This Session

> "If you want to market smarter and sell more stuff, you have to be able to answer: why do customers really buy?"

The answer is NOT demographic data. The answer is the progress they are trying to make.

## Framework 1: Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)

**Theory (Clayton Christensen, operationalized by Bob Moesta):**

People don't buy products. They *hire* products to help them make progress in a specific context.

```
PERSON HAS A JOB TO DO
        ↓
They face OBSTACLES preventing progress
        ↓
They HIRE a product to help get the job done
        ↓
If it works → they keep it, tell friends
If it doesn't → they FIRE it, look for alternative
```

**Critical distinction:**
> "It's not about what your product does. It's about what your customers can do *because of* your product."

**Applied to 100Hires:**

| Who | Job To Be Done | Obstacle | What they hire 100Hires for |
|---|---|---|---|
| SMB Founder | Hire the right person without it consuming my week | No system — everything lives in email and spreadsheets | An infrastructure that manages the process for them |
| Agency Recruiter | Fill more roles without adding headcount | Pipeline is fragmented across tools and memory | One place to see all candidates, all stages |
| HR Generalist | Manage 500 applications without drowning | Manual review with no prioritization | AI scoring that surfaces the top 5% automatically |

**Content rule derived:**
Every post that mentions 100Hires should describe the JOB, not the feature.

❌ "100Hires has AI candidate scoring"
✅ "Stop spending 8 hours reviewing resumes. AI scoring surfaces your top 5% in minutes."

## Framework 2: The Buying Journey Timeline

Every buyer follows a timeline:

```
1. TRIGGER EVENT
   Something happens that creates awareness of the problem
   (e.g. lost a candidate, failed a hire, opened 6 spreadsheets for one role)

2. PASSIVE LOOKING
   They start noticing things in their environment that might solve it
   (They scroll past a 100Hires LinkedIn post but don't click)

3. ACTIVE LOOKING
   Something pushes them to actually search for a solution
   (e.g. CEO asked for a hiring update they couldn't produce)

4. DECISION
   They evaluate options and choose
   (100Hires is already familiar — mere exposure effect)

5. EVALUATION
   Did I get my job done? Was it worth it?
   (This becomes the review, the referral, the LinkedIn post)
```

**What most B2B SaaS companies do wrong:**
They target only stage 3 (active looking) — people who are already searching. This is the most expensive and competitive stage.

**What the 100Hires LinkedIn strategy does:**
Target stages 1–2. Plant the brand during the passive looking stage so that when the trigger event pushes someone to active looking, 100Hires is already top of mind.

## Framework 3: The Trigger Event

**Definition:**
The moment a buyer first realizes they have a problem that needs solving.

**Trigger events for 100Hires ICP:**

| ICP | Trigger Event Examples |
|---|---|
| SMB Founder | Lost a great candidate to a faster company / Opened a spreadsheet and couldn't find who they'd spoken to |
| Agency Recruiter | Lost a placement because candidate fell through the cracks / Client complained about slow process |
| HR Generalist | Received 500 applications in 48 hours / CEO asked for hiring data they didn't have |

**Content derived from this:**
Posts that describe trigger events get stopped by people who are in the passive looking stage. They see themselves in the scenario — and remember 100Hires.

**Example post hook using trigger event framing:**
```
"You open your spreadsheet on Monday morning.
47 rows. 6 columns. Candidate statuses that nobody updated.
You can't remember if Sarah accepted the interview or just said she'd think about it.
You find out an hour later she's already taken another offer.

This is what losing great candidates looks like.
It's not dramatic. It's administrative."
```

## Framework 4: The ICP Interview Methodology (Clarity Calls)

**Core insight from Katelyn:**
> "One interview is worth a thousand surveys." — Des Traynor, Intercom co-founder

**Why surveys fail:**
95% of purchase decisions are driven by unconscious urges. Buyers cannot accurately describe their motivations in a survey. They can describe them in a story.

**The Clarity Call method:**
- Interview actual buyers (people who recently purchased your product OR a competitor's)
- Focus on their buying journey as a documentary — start to finish
- Never ask "what would you want?" — only ask "what did you do?"
- Extract language: their exact words become your content copy

**Questions that unlock the best content insights:**
1. "Walk me through the moment you first realized you needed something like this."
2. "What were you using before? What stopped working?"
3. "What almost stopped you from buying?"
4. "Who else did you consider? Why didn't you go with them?"
5. "What did it feel like when it actually worked?"

**For 100Hires:** Run 5 clarity calls with current users before building the next 3 months of content. The trigger events, the fears, and the identity aspirations they describe become the raw material for every pillar.

## Framework 5: Passive vs. Active Looking (SEO insight applicable to LinkedIn)

**Katelyn's keyword data example:**
- "How to wake up earlier" — 12,000 searches/month, low competition (passive lookers)
- "Twin bell alarm clock" — 600 searches/month, 19x more competitive (active lookers)

**LinkedIn translation:**
- Content about "how to hire faster" (passive looking, large audience, low competition) → 100Hires awareness posts
- Content about "best ATS software" (active looking, small audience, high competition) → not where LinkedIn wins

**Strategy conclusion:**
LinkedIn organic is a passive-looking channel. It builds brand and demand before buyers know they're buyers. Pair it with SEO/paid search (which captures active lookers) for full-funnel coverage.

## Framework 6: Top 3 Buyer Psychology Principles for Marketing

Katelyn's answer when asked for the top psychological principles:

**1. Trigger Events**
Understand what causes someone to enter the buying journey. Build content around those moments.

**2. Social Proof**
We trust people over companies. We especially trust people we already know. Build content that features customers (user-generated proof > brand claims).

**3. Mere Exposure Effect**
The more someone is exposed to a brand, the more they like and trust it. Coca-Cola spends 75% of revenue on marketing despite being the most recognized brand on the planet — because the effect never stops working.

**Implication for 100Hires:**
Show up. Every day. On LinkedIn. The first 20 posts won't generate leads. The 60th post will — because by then, the ICP has seen you enough times to feel familiar, and familiar = trustworthy = worth clicking.
