# Cross-Source Patterns — LinkedIn B2B SaaS Research
**Synthesized from:** 10 expert sources, 10 collected LinkedIn posts, 6+ video/podcast transcripts
**Status:** In progress — patterns will expand as more expert content is collected

## Pattern 1: The Hook Comes Before the Value — Always

**Sources:** Justin Welsh, Lara Acosta, Jasmin Alić, Katelyn Bourgoin
**Evidence:**
- Welsh: every post opens with a single punchy line that creates a curiosity gap before any value is delivered
- Acosta: hook formulas are treated as a separate craft — she maintains a "hook swipe file" and the SLAY framework starts with Story
- Alić: post anatomy explicitly names the hook as "line 1" — value only comes after the reader has been stopped
- Bourgoin (Post 01): Joshua Bell story functions entirely as a hook for 4 paragraphs before actionable advice

**B2B SaaS application:**
Never open with "At [Company], we believe..." or "Here are 5 tips..."

Always open with: a story, a contrarian statement, a quantified claim, or a named problem that stops the scroll.

**Template:**
```
"[Specific scenario the ICP recognizes] → [unexpected consequence] → [the lesson that reframes it]"
```

## Pattern 2: Zero-Click Is the Standard, Not the Exception

**Sources:** Amanda Natividad, Richard van der Blom, Ross Simmonds, Dave Gerhardt
**Evidence:**
- Natividad: co-invented the term — self-contained posts that build brand without requiring a link click
- van der Blom: algorithm data shows reach reduction when external links appear in the post body
- Simmonds: "distribute everywhere" means the LinkedIn post must stand alone — not a teaser for a blog
- Gerhardt: Exit Five posts deliver full value inside the post; links in comments only

**B2B SaaS application:**
Every post is the full article. Give the full insight in the post. Demo/trial links go in the first comment — never in the post body.

## Pattern 3: The Algorithm Rewards Depth of Relationship, Not Reach

**Sources:** Richard van der Blom, Jasmin Alić, Lara Acosta, Katelyn Bourgoin
**Evidence:**
- van der Blom: 2025 algorithm weights content from accounts you've recently interacted with
- Alić: comment strategy is an algorithm requirement, not a growth hack
- Acosta: "unconnected content" spreads through your network's engagement — your network IS your distribution system
- Bourgoin: mere exposure effect means the 20th impression from the same account beats the 1st from a viral post

**B2B SaaS application:**
Comment on 10 ICP posts per day before posting your own. Build relationships with 20–30 target accounts. Their engagement becomes your distribution floor.

## Pattern 4: Consistency Beats Virality — Every Time

**Sources:** Justin Welsh, Katelyn Bourgoin, Richard van der Blom, Lara Acosta
**Evidence:**
- Welsh: 1.5M followers through consistent daily posting, not viral moments
- Bourgoin: Coca-Cola still spends 75% of revenue on marketing because mere exposure never stops compounding
- van der Blom: algorithm rewards consistent posting schedules with higher baseline distribution
- Acosta: "Repeat yourself, please" — repetition builds reputation and algorithmic filing

**B2B SaaS application:**
Post 5 days per week for 90 days before evaluating results. The first 30 posts are infrastructure. Compounding starts at post 40+.

## Pattern 5: Naming the Problem Is Worth More Than Solving It

**Sources:** Katelyn Bourgoin, April Dunford, Chris Walker (archived)
**Evidence:**
- Bourgoin (Post 03): Chris Walker's "Dark Social" and "Measurement Gap" naming built his agency to acquisition
- Dunford: best-positioned companies own the language of their category
- Walker (archived): companies that name the competitive alternative own the consideration space

**B2B SaaS application:**
Coin a named problem your ICP recognizes. Use it consistently. When buyers search for that term and find your content — the Rumpelstiltskin Effect is working.

## Pattern 6: Identity Over Feature — Frame the Outcome as a Person Becoming

**Sources:** Katelyn Bourgoin, Justin Welsh, Lara Acosta, Dave Gerhardt
**Evidence:**
- Bourgoin: "Customers don't buy products because of who they are. They buy because of who they want to become."
- Welsh: sold the identity of "intentionally small = powerful," not content systems
- Acosta: audience follows her for "someone who built something from nothing"
- Gerhardt: Exit Five members pay to be part of a community of serious B2B marketers

**B2B SaaS application:**
Never write: "Our product automates email follow-ups."
Always write: "The companies that never ghost candidates don't have better recruiters. They have a system."

## Pattern 7: Distribution System Matters More Than Content Quality

**Sources:** Ross Simmonds, Amanda Natividad, Lara Acosta
**Evidence:**
- Simmonds: creation is 20% of the work, distribution is 80%
- Natividad: SparkToro built top-of-funnel with zero SEO by mastering dark social distribution
- Acosta: 0 to 300K was about mastering distribution mechanics (timing, comments, engagement)

**B2B SaaS application:**
Build the distribution system before optimizing content:
1. Identify 30 ICP accounts to engage daily
2. Establish posting schedule (5x/week, fixed times)
3. Build newsletter as email capture layer
4. Then optimize post quality based on performance data

## Pattern 8: Trust Channels Extend Beyond LinkedIn

**Sources:** Dave Gerhardt (Exit Five Live), Jana/Kmy email panelists, Amanda Natividad
**Evidence:**
- Gerhardt: Exit Five built $1M+ community from founder-led content → email → paid membership
- Jana: weekly newsletter cadence = trust touchpoint even when unopened
- Kmy: lurkers who never engage still accumulate trust through email touchpoints before buying
- Natividad: dark social measurement — value delivered in-channel, not via click-through

**B2B SaaS application:**
LinkedIn builds familiarity. Newsletter builds trust. Email sequences convert. Each channel has a distinct psychological job — don't collapse them into one.

## The Meta-Pattern: High-Performing B2B Content Does 3 Things

After analyzing collected posts and transcripts, every high-performing piece does exactly three things:

**1. Makes the reader feel seen** (they recognize their situation)
**2. Reframes their situation** (shows them what's really happening — the revelation)
**3. Points to a better version of themselves** (the identity they want to inhabit)

The product is mentioned last, if at all. The mechanism is psychological. The result is trust.

**Universal post template:**
```
"Here's what you're experiencing [makes them feel seen]
Here's what's actually causing it [the reframe]
Here's what the best teams do instead [points to identity]
[Optional: product makes this possible]"
```
