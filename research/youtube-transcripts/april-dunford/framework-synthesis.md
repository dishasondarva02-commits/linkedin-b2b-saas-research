## Core Frameworks

### 1. Five-Part Positioning Canvas
1. **Competitive alternatives** — What would customers do if you didn't exist?
2. **Unique attributes** — What do you have that alternatives don't?
3. **Value** — What value do those attributes enable?
4. **Target market characteristics** — Who cares most about that value?
5. **Market category** — What frame makes the value obvious?

### 2. Positioning-First Content Strategy
- Content fails when positioning is wrong — no amount of volume fixes unclear positioning
- Every post should reinforce ONE positioning statement
- If you can't state your positioning in one sentence, stop posting and fix positioning first

### 3. Competitive Alternative Analysis
- Don't position against competitors — position against the *status quo*
- For most B2B SaaS: the real competitor is spreadsheets, email, and manual processes
- Content that names the status quo as the enemy converts better than competitor comparison

### 4. "Obviously Awesome" Clarity Standard
- Would a buyer read your post and think "obviously, this is what I need"?
- If they have to work to understand why you're different, positioning is wrong

## Psychological Triggers

- **Cognitive ease:** Clear positioning = clear content = trust
- **Identity:** Market frame tells buyers what kind of person/company uses this product
- **Loss aversion:** Naming the status quo as costly ("spreadsheet hiring") creates urgency

---

## B2B SaaS Application

**Before writing content, complete the canvas:**
1. Alternative: "Spreadsheets + email + memory"
2. Unique: "One pipeline, AI scoring, automated follow-ups"
3. Value: "Hire faster without it consuming your week"
4. Target: "SMB founders and agency recruiters hiring 5–50 roles/year"
5. Category: "Modern hiring infrastructure for small teams"

**Every post should map to one of these five elements.** If it doesn't reinforce positioning, cut it.
